We Analyzed Over 500 Pitches: This is What Gets a VC Meeting

We built Feedback.vc as a way for founders to turn the frustrating black hole of fundraising into an informative exercise that helps them improve their pitches and the vision for their company. Participants get both quantitative and qualitative feedback across multiple aspects of their business.

We’ve processed over 500 double-blind pitches so far and looked to see what was driving success. Companies don’t submit anything personally identifiable about the founders or their business. They share what they do, not who they are (other than high-level team bios).

On the other side, an anonymous panel of VCs reviews every company. At the end, they have the option to opt into a direct introduction. The purpose of the platform is really organizing and standardizing the feedback for founders. The intros are a nice, but not guaranteed, side benefit.

We analyzed the results of our first 500 reviews and calculated the correlations between high marks in categories like team, problem area and business economics with the number of requested intros from our reviewing VCs. Overall, high scores on the review were a strong predictor of VC interest, but the results of the scores of the individual categories might surprise you.

Here’s what mattered—and what didn’t, in order of least to most predictive:

Product & Solution - Weak Predictor

It has been said that “ideas are a dime a dozen” and that seems to be the case with VCs. The investors who gave high marks on solutions only showed a slight interest in asking for a founder intro.

The Raise - Weak Predictor

Am I raising too little or too much? Am I going to signal something unintentionally based on what I’m asking for? It didn’t really really matter much to our investors. On many occasions, they looked past issues they had with the raise, seemingly because if they got interested in the company, they could influence what they saw as the right amount for the raise.

Team - Moderate Predictor

Team. Team. Team? Yeah, perhaps a good team isn’t everything. Some good teams got lots of intros and others, not so much. On the flip side, there were no good teams that got zero intros. Bad teams definitely sunk your chances—but having a great team didn’t necessarily mean lots of VCs wanted to meet you if they didn’t like the other aspects of your company.

The Basics & Gut Check - Moderate Predictor

We ask teams to to briefly describe what they’re working on, and then we ask VCs for a gut check on this high-level overview. This was a pretty decent predictor—which means that the next time you’re networking with VCs, if they don’t show interest after that first one liner, you might be better off politely excusing yourself than trying to stay and win them over.

Or, maybe get a more enticing one-liner.

Hypothesis - Moderate Predictor

Agreeing on how you see the world is important—but deciding what’s important about how you see the world is just as critical. We asked founders to state three beliefs that any investor who got to the finish line would have to agree with. Some founders who scored a lot of investor intros had high agreement with these stated principals. Others who scored high in this area didn’t get more than a couple of investors kicking the tires.

We can both agree that the sky is blue, but that doesn’t mean this will make money.

The Problem & Market Segment - Moderate to Strong Predictor

This would have been a stronger predictor had it not been for a few outliers at the top. Aside from a few companies who got a high number of intros despite scoring relatively low here, this was overall a very good predictor of who get intros. Picking the right problem to work on seemed more important than having the right solution by more than 2x.

So what was the most important factor?

Which area, if a VC only knew one thing about your company, was the most predictive of enticing a VC to want to meet?

Some founders might be disappointed, because its that age old question that they love to hate…

“How does it make money??”

Yes, the underlying economics of the business was the strongest predictor of whether a VC wanted an intro over any other aspect of the company. Founders who could clearly articulate an economic opportunity fared really well with investor interest.

Bonus: Gender Math

Our submissions remove any personally identifiable information about the founders—so our investors don’t know which teams have male or female founders. However, it was interesting that women, on average, scored 13% more intros on our platform. That may be because their scores were higher overall—especially in the team area, where they were voted 30% better than their male counterparts when the genders were removed.
